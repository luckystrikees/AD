Autistic Scholars

The Initiative

Autistic Scholars is an independent research initiative examining how different regulatory architectures of cognition interact within shared environments. The initiative develops a multi-level analytic framework that spans phenomenological description, computational formalization, physiological mechanism, molecular pathway, and ecological dynamics—treating these not as separate research areas but as interdependent levels of a single integrated argument.

The central claim is that contemporary psychology has implicitly adopted a particular architecture of social cognition as its normative baseline, and that this unexamined baseline shapes how cognitive variation is classified, interpreted, and responded to within scientific and institutional frameworks. The research program makes this baseline visible and traces the consequences of architectural mismatch from the level of social interaction through energetic cost, metabolic load, and long-term physiological outcome.

Methodologically, the initiative treats autistic lived experience as situated knowledge capable of informing theory generation and research design. Autistic perspectives function as analytic vantage points rather than objects of external observation. This orientation draws on participatory research traditions, neurodiversity scholarship, and the double empathy framework, and is informed by work in feminist epistemology, critical psychology, and disability studies.

Founding Scholar

Ericwilliam Brown is the founder and principal investigator of Autistic Scholars. He is a twice-exceptional, Black, gay, late-diagnosed autistic independent scholar whose research examines the epistemology of psychological classification, the regulatory architecture of social cognition, and the interaction dynamics between neurodivergent and neurotypical cognitive systems.

His work draws on predictive processing frameworks, philosophy of classification, social psychology, and autistic phenomenological discourse to develop formal models of how normative baselines become embedded within diagnostic categories. The current research program comprises five papers examining regulatory architecture across architectural, computational, experiential, mechanistic, and ecological levels of analysis.
