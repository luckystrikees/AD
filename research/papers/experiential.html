Working Paper III · Experiential

Structural Insolvency

Metabolic Sovereignty and the Bio-Political Economy
of Autistic Burnout

Research Program › III. Experiential

Author: Ericwilliam Brown

Status: Under Review

Abstract

Dominant clinical frameworks frequently mischaracterize autistic burnout as a psychological mood disorder or a failure of individual resilience. This manuscript reframes burnout as Structural Insolvency: a predictable physiological outcome of sustained metabolic extraction within social systems optimized for majority cognitive phenotypes. Integrating evidence on the bioenergetic vulnerability of parvalbumin-positive (PV+) interneurons with the BOLD Increases Approximate Prediction Error Metabolism (BIAPEM) hypothesis, we establish the intrinsically high metabolic baseline of high-fidelity autistic processing.

This analysis is calibrated through the author's positionality as a Systemic Witness—a Black, gay, late-diagnosed autistic male. This positionality functions not as biographical context, but as an active epistemological instrument, enabling detection of the specific prediction tax imposed by masking and the compound metabolic debt accrued through racialized illegibility and enforced double consciousness. We argue that the "weathering" observed in marginalized autistic populations represents the somatic accumulation of this unrecovered energy debt across the lifespan.

Finally, we posit that monotropism constitutes a necessary negentropic defense strategy rather than a pathological fixation. The manuscript concludes by calling for a paradigm shift from resilience-based interventions toward Metabolic Justice, including the validation of opacity, the provision of reparative rest, and the clinical auditing of systemic energy debt.

Keywords: autistic burnout, bioenergetics, structural insolvency, systemic witness, predictive coding, minority stress, metabolic justice, monotropism

Research Question

What does this cost the body?

Plain Language Summary

Burnout is a Hardware Crash

Clinical circles often treat autistic burnout like depression. This paper argues that's a dangerous mistake. Burnout is a metabolic bankruptcy. It occurs when a high-cost system (the autistic brain) is forced to spend more energy than it can possibly recover.

The "Tax" of Existing

Masking is Expensive: Maintaining a "performed self" alongside an "authentic self" creates a massive energy debt.

Weathering: Much like the chronic stress seen in other marginalized groups, the "weathering" of autistic bodies leads to accelerated physical decline. Studies have shown that autistic individuals face significantly higher rates of chronic health conditions and a lower life expectancy (sometimes estimated at 16 to 30 years shorter than the general population) due to systemic stress.

Metabolic Justice: We need to move from "building resilience" to "reparative rest."

The Path Forward

The author calls for Metabolic Justice:

Validate Opacity: The right to not perform or be "readable" to others.

Audit Energy Debt: Clinicians should measure the energy costs social systems impose on autistic people rather than asking the individual to "cope" better.

Monotropism as Self-Care: Intense focus on interests isn't a "pathological fixation" — it's an essential energy-conservation strategy.

Position in the Research Program

This paper takes the computational model of energetic asymmetry from Paper II and examines its lived, phenomenological consequences. It provides the experiential evidence that the mechanistic pathway in Paper IV must explain — showing what sustained metabolic cost looks and feels like from the inside.
