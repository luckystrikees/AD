Working Paper V · Ecological

Situating Distress

A Regulatory Ecology Framework for the
Autism–Trauma Overlap

Research Program › V. Ecological

Author: Ericwilliam Brown

Status: In preparation

Abstract

This paper provides the broadest integrative framework for the research program, connecting environmental input load, compensatory regulation, stress physiology, and regulatory flexibility in a dynamic systems model. It examines how overwhelming environments trigger a cascade: the brain works harder, the body gets stressed, and over time the capacity to cope degrades. The paper addresses the autism–trauma overlap, arguing that what is often diagnosed as trauma may in many cases be the cumulative effect of sustained architectural mismatch within non-calibrated environments.

Research Question

How do all these levels interact as a dynamic system?

Plain Language Summary

How do all these pieces fit together? This section looks at the big picture — how your brain, body, and environment interact as one system. It shows how overwhelming environments trigger a chain reaction: your brain works harder, your body gets stressed, and over time your ability to cope gets worn down. Understanding this cycle helps explain why autistic burnout happens.

Position in the Research Program

This paper integrates all preceding levels of analysis into a single dynamic systems model. It takes the architectural distinction (Paper I), the computational formalization (Paper II), the experiential evidence (Paper III), and the mechanistic pathway (Paper IV) and shows how they interact as a unified ecological system.
