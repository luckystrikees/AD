## Paper I: The Other Side of the Double Empathy Problem

**Why is this topic important?**

Psychology treats one way of thinking as the "normal" standard. Behaviors that differ from this standard are often labeled as deficits. But what if "normal" is just one specific style — not the only right way? This paper shows that what we call normal social thinking is a particular cognitive architecture, not a universal default. Making this visible changes how we understand autism.

**What is the purpose of this article?**

This article identifies two distinct ways people process social information: socially coupled cognition (stabilized through shared emotional signals) and internally mediated cognition (stabilized through internal predictive models). It shows how treating one style as the standard has shaped autism diagnosis. The goal is to reframe diagnostic categories as perspective-dependent descriptions rather than neutral characterizations of impairment.

**What personal or professional perspectives do the authors bring to this topic?**

The author is a twice-exceptional, Black, gay, late-diagnosed autistic independent scholar. Decades of observing majority social behavior from within a different cognitive architecture allowed the author to identify patterns most people take for granted. This lived experience is an analytical tool, not just personal background.

**What is already known about this topic?**

Research shows that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people arise from mutual differences in how social signals are interpreted — the double empathy problem. Studies show autistic people communicate with each other as effectively as non-autistic people do with each other. Autistic online communities have long described patterns of majority social behavior using analytical language mirroring clinical descriptions of autism.

**What do the authors recommend?**

Psychology should examine its own hidden assumptions with the same rigor it applies to studying human variation. The two cognitive architectures should be recognized as different regulatory strategies, not normal versus deficient. Environments should accommodate both architectures rather than being calibrated to only one.

**How will these recommendations help autistic adults now or in the future?**

This would shift how society views autistic people — from broken versions of normal to people with different but equally valid ways of processing the world. This could reduce stigma, improve diagnostic accuracy, and lead to more inclusive schools, workplaces, and healthcare settings.

## Paper II: Metabolic Gating in Social Interaction

**Why is this topic important?**

In many social interactions, one person does more of the work to keep things going. Existing explanations attribute this to differences in motivation, personality, or social roles. But this paper shows that unequal effort can emerge even when both people start with the same motivation. The cause is not psychological — it is energetic.

**What is the purpose of this article?**

This article proposes that unequal adaptive effort arises from the brain's energetic limits on how sensitively it processes social information. Staying attuned to a partner requires sustained attention to the gap between what you expect and what happens. This attention is physiologically costly. When two people differ in their capacity to sustain this processing, one reaches their limit first and reduces attention. The other absorbs the full burden. The paper formalizes this and makes specific predictions about when this shift happens.

**What personal or professional perspectives do the authors bring to this topic?**

The author is an autistic independent scholar whose research examines the regulatory architecture of social cognition. The author's experience of navigating social environments not calibrated for their cognitive architecture provides direct insight into the energetic costs of sustained social attunement.

**What is already known about this topic?**

Research on relationships shows partners with more to lose tend to accommodate more. Studies on emotional labor show certain roles — caregivers, therapists — carry disproportionate responsibility for managing emotional dynamics. Communication research shows people adjust their style based on social identity and status. But all these explanations require that the person doing less work has changed their motivation. This paper identifies a pathway to the same outcome without any change in motivation.

**What do the authors recommend?**

Researchers and clinicians should recognize energetic constraints on social processing as a real factor in interactional asymmetry. Future research should measure how social sensitivity changes over time during interactions. Clinical approaches should account for the metabolic cost of sustained social attunement rather than treating disengagement as a character flaw.

**How will these recommendations help autistic adults now or in the future?**

This validates that social fatigue is a real physiological phenomenon, not a personal failing. It could change how therapists, educators, and employers understand social challenges — shifting from blame to accommodation. It could lead to practical supports such as structured breaks during demanding interactions and recognition that some interactions are inherently more costly than others.

## Paper III: Structural Insolvency

**Why is this topic important?**

Autistic burnout is often misunderstood as depression or a failure of individual resilience. This paper argues this is a fundamental error. Burnout is not a software problem — it is a hardware problem. It is a physiological crisis that occurs when a high-cost system is asked to produce more energy than it can sustain.

**What is the purpose of this article?**

This article reframes autistic burnout as "structural insolvency" — a predictable physiological outcome of sustained metabolic extraction within social systems designed for majority cognitive patterns. It brings together evidence about the high energy cost of autistic brain processing with the lived experience of masking. It argues that the accelerated physical decline observed in marginalized autistic populations represents accumulated energy debt from a lifetime of compensation.

**What personal or professional perspectives do the authors bring to this topic?**

The author is a Black, gay, late-diagnosed autistic man who describes himself as a "systemic witness." His lived experience functions as a metabolic sensor — he can detect the specific energy cost of social interactions invisible to conventional research. His experience of maintaining two concurrent cognitive streams — the authentic self and the performed self — provides direct evidence of the metabolic price of masking.

**What is already known about this topic?**

Research shows the autistic brain maintains a precise balance between excitatory and inhibitory signals that is metabolically expensive. Specific brain cells called parvalbumin-positive interneurons require substantial energy to maintain rapid firing patterns needed for precise information processing. Research on minority stress shows marginalized populations experience accelerated physical decline due to chronic stress.

**What do the authors recommend?**

A shift from resilience-based interventions toward metabolic justice. This includes validating opacity — the right of autistic people to not be transparent to others. Providing reparative rest — recognizing rest as a physiological necessity, not a luxury. Clinical auditing of systemic energy debt — measuring and addressing the metabolic cost social systems impose on autistic people. Recognizing monotropism as a necessary energy-conservation strategy rather than a pathological fixation.

**How will these recommendations help autistic adults now or in the future?**

Instead of being told to build resilience, autistic adults would have their metabolic needs recognized and accommodated. Clinical practice would shift from pathologizing burnout to auditing the systemic conditions that produce it. Policy could address structural factors creating metabolic debt — workplace demands for constant social performance, educational environments requiring sustained masking, and healthcare systems misdiagnosing physiological crisis as psychological disorder.
