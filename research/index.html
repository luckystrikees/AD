Research Program

Regulatory Architectures
and the Energetic Limits
of Social Adaptation

This research program develops an integrated account of cognitive regulatory architecture, spanning phenomenological identification, computational formalization, metabolic mechanism, and ecological dynamics. The work operates across five interdependent levels of analysis, tracing the consequences of architectural mismatch within environments calibrated to a single norm. It also examines how these asymmetries are reproduced and stabilised within cultural and representational systems.

⁂

I. Architectural

Identifies the regulatory architecture of majority social cognition as a specific system rather than an unexamined default. Examines how this architecture functions as the implicit normative baseline within psychological classification.

Question: What are the two architectures?

Working Paper: The Other Side of the Double Empathy Problem: Making Majority Social Cognition Visible as a Specific Regulatory Architecture

Status: Submitted for publication. Preprint available on PsyArXiv (opens in a new tab).

II. Computational

Formalizes the architectural distinction within predictive processing and active inference frameworks. Introduces precision weighting as metabolically constrained and develops the concept of dyadic energetic asymmetry.

Question: How do energetic constraints shape the dynamics of social inference?

Working Paper: Metabolic Gating in Social Interaction: An Energetic Account of Asymmetric Adaptation

Status: Under Review. Preprint available on zenodo (opens in a new tab).

III. Experiential

Examines the lived experience of sustained regulatory load. Links energetic cost to intersectional identity, examining how chronic compensatory regulation compounds across racialized, gendered, and neurological axes of normative categorization.

Question: What does this cost the body?

Working Paper: Structural Insolvency: Metabolic Sovereignty and the Bio-Political Economy of Autistic Burnout

Status: Under Review.

IV. Mechanistic

Proposes a specific neuro-immunometabolic pathway linking chronic compensatory regulation to mitochondrial dysfunction. Provides falsifiable biomarker predictions and a proposed study design.

Question: What is the specific molecular pathway?

Working Paper: Neuroimmune Metabolic Collapse in Autistic Burnout

Status: In preparation.

V. Ecological

Provides the broadest integrative framework, connecting environmental input load, compensatory regulation, stress physiology, and regulatory flexibility in a dynamic systems model.

Question: How do all these levels interact as a dynamic system?

Working Paper: Situating Distress: A Regulatory Ecology Framework for the Autism–Trauma Overlap

Status: In preparation.

Cultural / Representational Extension
(Cross-Level)

Examines how cognitive regulatory architectures are encoded, enforced, and naturalized within narrative and media systems. Treats cultural forms as ecological environments that structure the distribution of interpretive authority, shaping whose cognition is legible, credible, and governable.

Question: How are cognitive architectures encoded and governed within cultural narratives?

Application: Managed Difference: Neurotypical Mediation and the Conditional Legibility of Twice-Exceptional Characters on Television

Status: In preparation.

❦

Program Structure

These lines of enquiry are structurally interdependent. The architectural distinction identified phenomenologically at Level I is formalized computationally at Level II as differential precision weighting under energetic constraint. The dyadic energetic asymmetry introduced at Level II provides the formal mechanism whose long-term metabolic consequences are described at Level III. The metabolic insolvency documented at Level III is specified at the molecular level at Level IV. Level V provides the systems-level framework within which all preceding mechanisms operate, connecting the architectural mismatch described at Level I to the environmental input load that initiates the regulatory cascade.

Finally, the cross-level extensions apply this integrated framework to broader sociocultural ecosystems, demonstrating how the normative baselines identified at the foundation of this architecture are continuously encoded, governed, and naturalized within institutional structures and cultural narratives.
