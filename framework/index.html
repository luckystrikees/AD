Research Program

Regulatory Architecture
and the Energetic Limits
of Social Adaptation

A multi-level account of how different regulatory architectures of cognition interact within shared environments.

This research program develops an integrated account of cognitive regulatory architecture, from the phenomenological identification of distinct social-cognitive systems through their computational formalization, physiological consequences, molecular mechanisms, and ecological dynamics. The five papers constitute a single argument developed across different explanatory levels, each answering a successive question about what happens when different regulatory architectures interact within environments calibrated to a single norm.

I. Architectural

The Other Side of the Double Empathy Problem: Making Majority Social Cognition Visible as a Specific Regulatory Architecture

Makes the majority cognitive architecture visible as a specific regulatory system rather than an unexamined default. Identifies socially coupled cognition and internally mediated cognition as distinct regulatory architectures. Grounds the analysis in early autistic community epistemology and empirical social psychology. Extends Milton's (2012) double empathy framework by providing a mechanistic account of the majority architecture.

Question: What are the two architectures?

Status: Submitted for publication. Preprint available on PsyArXiv.

II. Computational

Energetic Precision Regulation

Formalizes the architectural distinction within predictive processing and active inference frameworks. Introduces precision weighting as metabolically constrained and develops the concept of dyadic energetic asymmetry. Establishes the theoretical machinery at the level of general computational neuroscience, making the framework applicable to any coupled inference system under energetic constraint.

Question: What happens computationally when the two architectures interact?

Status: In preparation.

III. Experiential

Structural Insolvency

Applies the metabolic framework to the lived experience of sustained regulatory load. Introduces the Systemic Witness methodology. Links energetic cost to intersectional identity, examining how chronic compensatory regulation compounds across racialized, gendered, and neurological axes of normative categorization.

Question: What does this cost the body?

Status: In preparation.

IV. Mechanistic

Functional Iron Blockade

Proposes a specific neuro-immunometabolic pathway linking chronic compensatory regulation to mitochondrial dysfunction. Maps the cascade from tonic noradrenergic upregulation through inflammatory signaling to iron sequestration via the hepcidin-ferroportin axis. Provides falsifiable biomarker predictions and a proposed study design.

Question: What is the specific molecular pathway?

Status: In preparation.

V. Ecological

Regulatory Ecology

Provides the broadest integrative framework, connecting environmental input load, compensatory regulation, stress physiology, and regulatory flexibility in a dynamic systems model. Situates all preceding levels within an ecological account of how regulatory architectures interact with environmental structure across time.

Question: How do all these levels interact as a dynamic system?

Status: In preparation.

Program Structure

The papers are structurally interdependent. The architectural distinction identified phenomenologically at Level I is formalized computationally at Level II as differential precision weighting under energetic constraint. The dyadic energetic asymmetry introduced at Level II provides the formal mechanism whose long-term metabolic consequences are described at Level III. The metabolic insolvency documented at Level III is specified at the molecular level at Level IV. Level V provides the systems-level framework within which all preceding mechanisms operate, connecting the architectural mismatch described at Level I to the environmental input load that initiates the regulatory cascade.
